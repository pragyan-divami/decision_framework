# Scenario: The Tenova Variation Order Cascade
## Cross-cutting Commercial & Contract Variance Scenario — All 10 Personas

**Scenario date:** October 2026 — 15 months into construction, 14 months from commissioning target
**Classification:** Contracts & Commercial Variance
**Trigger:** Single contract event cascading across all project roles

---

## The event

On 14 October 2026, Tenova's contract manager formally submits **Variation Order VO-112** to Tata Steel UK's project director. This is not a routine change request. VO-112 is a claim under the EPC contract's Force Majeure and Exceptional Cost Escalation clauses, citing three compounding factors that Tenova argues were outside both parties' reasonable contemplation at contract execution in October 2024:

**Factor 1 — Steel and fabrication cost inflation.**
The structural steel required for the EAF vessel support structure and gantry has increased 34% above the contract's embedded material cost assumptions, driven by a combination of EU steel tariff retaliation measures and post-British-Steel-nationalisation market disruption in UK structural steel supply. Tenova's procurement team locked in prices for 60% of the structural steel at contract rates; the remaining 40% — approximately 1,800 tonnes — must now be purchased at current market rates.

**Factor 2 — Specialist labour scarcity premium.**
The pool of certified EAF refractory installation engineers across Europe has tightened severely as three simultaneous major EAF construction projects (Port Talbot, a French greenfield, and a Polish EAF upgrade) compete for the same 200 or so specialists globally. Tenova's refractory subcontractor is demanding a 28% labour rate increase above the subcontract price to retain the team through the Port Talbot installation phase.

**Factor 3 — Revised off-gas treatment specification.**
UK Environment Agency issued revised particulate emission limits in August 2026 that tighten PM2.5 discharge standards for large EAF installations by 40%. The filter specification in the current Tenova design does not meet the new standard. A full fume treatment plant redesign and upgraded filtration system is required — this was not contemplated in the original contract specification.

**Total VO-112 claim: £31.4 million additional cost, plus an 11-week programme extension.**

The breakdown:
- Structural steel escalation: £8.7M
- Refractory labour premium: £6.2M
- Fume treatment plant redesign and upgrade: £16.5M
- Associated programme impact cost (11-week extension): embedded in above

Tenova's legal position: all three factors qualify under the Force Majeure / Exceptional Cost Escalation clause and Tata Steel UK is contractually obligated to meet the additional cost and accept the programme extension.

Tata Steel UK's initial legal position: Factor 1 (steel inflation) is a general market risk that Tenova should have priced into contingency; Factor 2 (labour scarcity) is a consequence of Tenova's own commercial risk in accepting multiple simultaneous projects; Factor 3 (Environment Agency specification change) is a legitimate contract variation — Tata accepts this element in principle.

**Agreed in principle:** £16.5M for Factor 3 (fume treatment redesign).
**Disputed:** £14.9M for Factors 1 and 2 — both parties have instructed lawyers.

**The immediate programme decision:** Regardless of the commercial dispute resolution, the work must proceed. Tenova has the right to suspend Factor 1 and 2 works pending payment under the contract's payment security clause — which would add an estimated 6–8 weeks to the slip if invoked. Tata must decide within 21 days whether to pay the disputed £14.9M under protest (to keep the programme moving) or withhold and risk the suspension.

## Why this hits all ten personas

This is not a technical problem or a stakeholder management problem — it is a **commercial and contractual failure** that simultaneously touches every domain on the project:

- The disputed money must come from somewhere (**P4 CFO**)
- The programme extension threatens the 2027 commissioning date and the grant covenant (**P1 CEO, P3 Project Director, P6 Board Chair**)
- The fume treatment redesign must pass new Environment Agency standards before the EAF can operate — which is a planning condition (**P3 Project Director**)
- The labour scarcity claim reflects the same market that is making P2's EAF operator reskilling harder (**P2 CHRO**)
- The 11-week slip, if confirmed, changes what Kate can commit to scrap suppliers (**P7 Scrap Procurement**)
- An 11-week slip pushes National Grid's infrastructure synchronisation into narrower margin (**P8 Energy**)
- Priya Mehta's customer commitments to BMW and JLR depend on the commissioning date holding (**P10 Commercial Director**)
- The dispute becomes a Transition Board agenda item — Alun Davies and Steve Hunt will be told (**P5 Union, P9 Council**)
- The board must decide whether VO-112 constitutes a material event requiring regulatory or investor disclosure (**P6 Board Chair**)

## Per-persona decisions

### P1 · Rajesh Nair (CEO)
**Decision: Pay under protest or withhold and risk suspension?**

Tata's lawyers say withholding is defensible on Factors 1 and 2. But invoking the payment security clause would give Tenova the right to suspend construction for 6–8 weeks — and that suspension would become public. Rajesh must decide within 21 days.

**Options:**
- **A — Pay the full £31.4M under protest.** Programme continues uninterrupted. Legal dispute proceeds in parallel through the contract's dispute resolution mechanism. Risk: sets a precedent; the £14.9M may be unrecoverable if the dispute goes against Tata.
- **B — Pay only the agreed £16.5M (Factor 3).** Dispute £14.9M through formal contract mechanism. Tenova may invoke suspension. Risk: programme slips 6–8 weeks on top of the 11-week extension; total slip could be 17–19 weeks; 2027 commissioning date is gone.
- **C — Negotiate a commercial settlement.** Split the difference — pay £23–25M as a full and final settlement, close the dispute, and keep Tenova cooperative for the remainder of the programme. Risk: paying more than Tata's legal position requires; sets precedent for future claims.

**Tension:** Programme continuity and 2027 date vs fiduciary discipline and legal precedent.
**Time horizon:** 21-day decision window; legal dispute could run 12–18 months.
**KPIs:** K1 (schedule), K2 (budget), K3 (grant covenant), K7 (customer offtake commitments).

### P2 · Chris Jaques (CHRO)
**Decision: Does the refractory labour crisis affect the EAF operator reskilling programme — and do you use it to make the IJmuiden secondment case?**

The same refractory specialist shortage Tenova cited in VO-112 Factor 2 is being felt in Chris's reskilling pipeline — the external EAF training providers he was planning to use have had to delay Tata's cohort because their own trainers are committed to the same three simultaneous EAF projects. The reskilling timeline was already tight; the trainer availability crisis means the next scheduled cohort is now 8 weeks behind.

**Options:**
- **A — Escalate the trainer availability problem to the CEO as a commissioning risk.** Force a formal programme board decision on whether to mandate the IJmuiden secondments now, using the VO-112 evidence of industry-wide specialist scarcity as the justification. Turns a reskilling logistics problem into a strategic argument.
- **B — Absorb the 8-week delay quietly and compress later cohorts.** Manage the problem internally without escalating. Risk: each week of delay at this stage is harder to recover; the first cohort sets the pace for all subsequent ones.
- **C — Fast-track a direct hire of refractory specialists as EAF operator trainers.** Offer the same refractory engineers cited in Tenova's claim a parallel training role at Tata — they are on-site anyway and have the exact expertise the reskilling programme needs. Cost: ~£850K for a 6-month training facilitation contract.

**Tension:** Protecting the reskilling pipeline vs using the crisis as leverage for a structural decision the CEO needs to make anyway.
**Time horizon:** Next cohort starts in 6 weeks; decision needed in 10 days.
**KPIs:** K1 (EAF-ready headcount), K2 (graduation rate), K7 (training cost per operator).

### P3 · Dr. Owen Griffiths (Project Director)
**Decision: How to manage programme continuity during a live commercial dispute with the primary contractor?**

Owen is contractually obligated to continue issuing instructions to Tenova, but Tenova's site team is operating under their own legal counsel's guidance — which means every instruction Owen issues is now being logged and potentially reserved as supporting evidence in the dispute. The site atmosphere has changed. Tenova's senior site manager has told Owen privately that if the £14.9M is not paid, the refractory team will be redeployed to the French EAF project within 30 days.

**Options:**
- **A — Operate the site formally under contract, documenting everything.** Issue all instructions in writing, require written acknowledgement. Protects Tata's legal position but slows decision velocity on site from hours to days. Risk: construction programme suffers from the bureaucratic overhead even before any formal suspension.
- **B — Maintain informal working relationship with Tenova's site team while dispute is resolved at commercial level.** Keep site velocity up; deal with the legal dispute through corporate channels without letting it infect the construction team relationship. Risk: informal working during a live dispute creates ambiguity about what has been agreed and for how much — any deviation becomes a new variation claim.
- **C — Accelerate parallel works that are not affected by the refractory dispute.** Shift resources to the scrap handling facility, LMF installation, and electrical infrastructure while the Tenova dispute is active. Partially decouples programme velocity from the dispute outcome. Risk: resource diversion may create quality risks on the accelerated work; ABB's schedule is not designed for acceleration.

**Tension:** Site velocity and team relationships vs legal protection during an active commercial dispute.
**Time horizon:** 30-day refractory team redeployment threat; programme board meets in 5 days.
**KPIs:** K1 (schedule variance), K4 (contractor performance score), K7 (scope change orders).

### P4 · Sian Morgan (CFO)
**Decision: Where does £31.4M come from — and what does paying it do to the grant covenant?**

The capital programme budget has a 7.5% contingency built in — approximately £93M against the £1.25B programme. VO-112 at £31.4M is 33% of that contingency. If Tata pays the full amount, 33% of its total programme contingency is consumed by a single contractor dispute at month 15 of a 29-month construction programme. There are 14 months left and multiple other programme risks still live.

Additionally, the UK Government grant is milestone-linked. Sian needs to assess whether the budget overrun triggers a covenant disclosure obligation — and whether it changes DBET's risk assessment of the programme.

**Options:**
- **A — Approve payment from contingency, notify DBET proactively.** Full transparency; maintains grant relationship. Risk: DBET may require a formal programme review before releasing the next milestone payment — which could delay cash flow by 8–12 weeks.
- **B — Approve payment from contingency, do not notify DBET unless required.** Only disclose if the overrun breaches a defined threshold in the grant agreement. Risk: if DBET later discovers Tata knew and didn't tell them, the relationship damage is severe.
- **C — Reject the full payment; reserve contingency for the agreed Factor 3 element only.** Do not consume contingency on disputed items. If Tenova suspends, the 6–8 week slip is a budget problem for a different period. Risk: programme slip costs are in some ways more expensive than the disputed amount — the indirect cost of a 6–8 week suspension (slab import extension, customer risk, staff costs) may exceed £14.9M.

**Tension:** Cash discipline and contingency preservation vs programme continuity and grant transparency.
**Time horizon:** 21-day payment decision; grant covenant review cycle is quarterly.
**KPIs:** K1 (grant drawdown compliance), K2 (capital spend vs budget), K7 (debt and working capital), K8 (grant clawback risk).

### P5 · Alun Davies (National Officer, Community Union)
**Decision: Take VO-112 to the Transition Board or keep it internal to Tata?**

Alun will be briefed on VO-112 through his Transition Board membership. The 11-week programme extension means the EAF commissioning date is now formally at risk. This directly affects the timeline for EAF operator job creation — the moment redundant former steelworkers had been told to look forward to. The refractory labour claim (Factor 2) also highlights that Tenova is using non-local specialists for a key construction phase while Community Union has been pushing for more local hiring.

**Options:**
- **A — Request a formal Transition Board agenda item on VO-112 and its workforce implications.** Forces public transparency; puts the commissioning delay and the non-local specialist labour question on the record. Risk: antagonises Tata at the worst possible time — when Tata needs contractor goodwill and the union needs EAF operator terms cooperation.
- **B — Accept the private briefing and monitor without escalating.** Trust the process; wait for the commercial dispute to resolve before making it a Transition Board issue. Risk: if the delay hardens and former workers who were counting on 2027 EAF jobs find out Alun knew and said nothing, it is a repeat of the blast furnace closure communications failure.
- **C — Raise it quietly through Stephen Kinnock (MP) as a parliamentary question.** Creates political pressure without directly confronting Tata; forces DBET to publicly confirm the programme status. Risk: parliamentary questions are public record; this is effectively option A but less controllable.

**Tension:** Institutional accountability to members vs cooperative relationship needed for EAF operator terms.
**Time horizon:** Transition Board meets in 3 weeks; parliamentary question window is now.
**KPIs:** K2 (member trust), K3 (transition fund disbursement), K4 (EAF operator wage negotiation status), K5 (formal escalations).

### P6 · Dr. Henrik Adam (Board Chair)
**Decision: Is VO-112 a material event requiring regulatory or investor disclosure?**

The Tata Steel UK board must assess whether £31.4M in disputed contract variation, plus an 11-week programme extension, constitutes a material event under UK company law, the grant agreement's disclosure obligations, or Tata Steel Ltd's listing obligations on the Indian stock exchanges.

The independent board members (Anne Jessopp CBE, Jacqui Murray, Sarah Williams-Gardener) are already asking. The audit committee wants a legal opinion. Henrik must advise the board on what to disclose, when, and to whom.

**Options:**
- **A — Treat VO-112 as a material event and issue a proactive disclosure to DBET, Welsh Government, and Tata Steel Ltd's Indian exchange filings.** Full transparency; protects independent directors. Risk: disclosure triggers a media story — "Tata Steel Port Talbot EAF £31M over budget and 11 weeks late" is a damaging headline 14 months from commissioning.
- **B — Treat VO-112 as within normal programme contingency and not material — disclose only if and when the commercial dispute is resolved against Tata.** Programme-level decision; not a board-level material event. Risk: if the dispute escalates and it later emerges the board classified it as immaterial, independent director liability is real.
- **C — Commission a legal opinion on materiality and delay disclosure pending its conclusion.** Buys 2–3 weeks; ensures the board has defensible grounds for its decision. Risk: the delay itself may become an issue if DBET or Tata Group discovers VO-112 independently in the interim.

**Tension:** Board governance and director liability vs the media and political consequences of formal disclosure.
**Time horizon:** Legal opinion: 2–3 weeks; Indian exchange filing obligations: 48 hours if material.
**KPIs:** K1 (grant covenant compliance), K2 (board governance health), K5 (reputational index), K6 (EAF project health).

### P7 · Kate Ellison (Head of Scrap Procurement)
**Decision: Does an 11-week commissioning slip change the scrap contract strategy?**

Kate's current contracting strategy assumed EAF commissioning in Q4 2027 and first full production in Q1 2028. Her long-term scrap contracts are structured around a January 2028 first delivery date. An 11-week slip pushes first production to approximately March–April 2028.

This creates a contractual problem: three of the larger scrap supply agreements she was about to sign have a "first delivery no later than February 2028" clause, after which the merchant can terminate without penalty. If commissioning slips to March–April, those contracts void automatically.

**Options:**
- **A — Renegotiate the three at-risk contracts now, before the slip is formally confirmed.** Tell the merchants the start date may move by 8–12 weeks; ask for a grace period clause in exchange for a modest price concession (~£2/tonne). Cost: ~£1.4M/yr on those contracts. Risk: if the programme recovers and commissioning holds at Q4 2027, Tata has paid a concession it didn't need to.
- **B — Wait for the VO-112 dispute to resolve before renegotiating.** Do not trigger a conversation with scrap merchants about a delay that has not been formally confirmed. Risk: by the time VO-112 is resolved (6–8 weeks), the merchants' own pricing windows may have closed — and renegotiating under time pressure will cost more.
- **C — Treat the three at-risk contracts as expendable and replace them with shorter-duration bridge contracts.** Accept the loss and sign 12-month spot-priced bridge agreements to cover the delay period. Cost: PSX spot prices are currently running ~£12/tonne above long-term contract rates. Risk: further delays compound the spot exposure; bridge contracts create a rolling renegotiation burden.

**Tension:** Contract certainty vs the cost of acting on an unconfirmed programme slip.
**Time horizon:** Three scrap contracts have February 2028 termination clauses; renegotiation takes 4–6 weeks.
**KPIs:** K1 (contracted volume), K2 (contract price vs PSX index), K6 (EAF scrap cost component), K7 (market concentration risk).

### P8 · James Whitfield (Head of Energy)
**Decision: Does the 11-week slip invalidate the National Grid synchronisation agreement?**

National Grid committed to deliver the new high-voltage infrastructure to Port Talbot by end-2027 — aligned to the original EAF commissioning target. An 11-week slip moves Tata's requirement to approximately February–March 2028. This is actually positive for National Grid's programme (they now have more time), but it creates a contractual ambiguity: the current agreement specifies "ready by end 2027" — if Tata's commissioning moves, does the National Grid obligation move too, and does it require a formal contract amendment?

Separately, the renewable PPA signed at £68/MWh was structured with a "first EAF draw" trigger date of Q4 2027. If first draw moves to Q1 2028, the PPA's pricing period starts 11 weeks later — which James needs to confirm is consistent with the wind farm's own operational milestones.

**Options:**
- **A — Issue a formal contract amendment notice to National Grid and the PPA counterparty.** Updates both agreements to reflect the revised timeline. Risk: formal amendments trigger renegotiation windows — National Grid may use the amendment to revise terms; the PPA counterparty may request price renegotiation on an agreement that is currently below market.
- **B — Do nothing contractually; treat both agreements as flexible within a 3-month window.** Most infrastructure contracts have implicit flexibility for minor schedule adjustments. Risk: if the slip exceeds the informal flexibility window and either party invokes strict contract terms, Tata is in breach — a worse position than having amended proactively.
- **C — Issue informal notice letters to both parties — acknowledging the potential slip, requesting confirmation of continued alignment, without triggering a formal amendment process.** Preserves optionality; puts both parties on notice without opening renegotiation. Risk: legally ambiguous — informal notice may or may not constitute sufficient notification under the contract terms.

**Tension:** Contract security vs avoiding renegotiation of agreements that are currently on good terms.
**Time horizon:** PPA pricing trigger is Q4 2027 — formal amendment needs to be in place 8 weeks before trigger.
**KPIs:** K1 (electricity cost), K4 (PPA terms), K6 (National Grid delivery), K5 (green certification readiness).

### P9 · Cllr Steve Hunt (Council Leader)
**Decision: How to communicate the VO-112 delay to a community still processing the redundancies?**

The 11-week commissioning slip means the EAF jobs that people in Port Talbot were told to look forward to in "late 2027" now arrive in early 2028. For a town that lost 2,500 jobs and ~£200M in annual earnings, an 11-week slip is not a technical detail — it is another piece of bad news landing on a community that is exhausted by bad news.

Cllr Hunt also needs to decide whether the VO-112 contract dispute changes anything about his council's planning condition enforcement obligations — specifically whether the revised fume treatment specification (Factor 3 of VO-112) affects the planning conditions his committee approved in February 2025.

**Options:**
- **A — Proactively communicate the slip to the community, framed as responsible project management.** Hold a community briefing; explain the fume treatment upgrade as a genuine environmental improvement (which it is — PM2.5 limits have been tightened). Turn the negative into a "we're doing this right" message. Risk: some residents and media will lead with "EAF delayed again" regardless of framing.
- **B — Say nothing until Tata makes a formal announcement.** The slip is Tata's to announce; Steve should not pre-empt. Risk: if it leaks first through a contractor or ITV Wales, Steve looks like he was hiding it — damaging community trust already strained by the redundancy process.
- **C — Request a Transition Board extraordinary session to discuss the implications.** Formally records that the council acted immediately on receiving the information. Risk: an extraordinary Transition Board session makes VO-112 a political event before the commercial dispute is even resolved.

**Tension:** Community trust and transparency vs not getting ahead of a commercial dispute that may resolve in Tata's favour.
**Time horizon:** Community patience is thin; media will find out within 4–6 weeks.
**KPIs:** K6 (resident trust), K4 (business survival rate), K2 (jobs protected), K7 (eligibility disputes).

Show customers that Tata manages transparency well. Risk: if BMW finds out Tata told JLR and not them, the commercial relationship damage is severe — it looks like Tata protects the related party and manages everyone else.

## The meta-tension for the decision framework

Every persona faces a version of the same underlying problem: **act on information that has not been officially confirmed, or wait for confirmation and lose the ability to act proactively.**

The commercial dispute has not been resolved. The programme slip has not been formally issued. The board has not made a materiality determination. But the world is already moving — scrap contracts have termination clauses counting down, BMW's intelligence network is already active, former steelworkers are already asking their union rep questions, and the PPA pricing trigger is only 14 months away.

This is the scenario type where emotion state most sharply differentiates decisions: Cautious and Analytical will want confirmation before acting; Decisive and Strategic will act on the signal; Diplomatic will try to manage information flow without triggering reactions; Pragmatic will triage — act on the bits that are time-critical and wait on the rest.
