## Scenario: Supply Chain Transition Fund — 40 businesses waiting, money running out, criteria too narrow

The UK Government's Supply Chain Transition Fund has £30M committed (doubled in December 2024). By April 2026, 40 businesses employing approximately 2,000 people have begun the application process. However, the fund is processing slowly: only £8.2M has been disbursed, protecting approximately 390 jobs. At the current burn rate, the fund will be exhausted by October 2026 — before the majority of supply chain redundancies have been processed (most expected in 2025–2026).

Steve Hunt faces a specific problem: the eligibility threshold (30% revenue exposure to Tata, reduced from 50% in December 2024) is still too narrow for approximately 15 businesses that had 20–29% Tata exposure but have seen their Tata revenue disappear entirely because of supply chain restructuring downstream of the redundancies.

Three options:

**Option A — Escalate to the Transition Board requesting a second reduction in threshold to 20% and additional funding.** Bring it as a formal agenda item with a costed proposal (estimated additional: £8–10M to cover the 15 excluded businesses). Risk: UK Government has already doubled the fund once; a second ask may be read as poor financial management; ministers may say no at a politically awkward time.

**Option B — Create a parallel Welsh Government fund to cover the gap.** Work through Welsh Government Cabinet Secretary Rebecca Evans to create a supplementary Welsh fund for businesses in the 20–29% exposure band. Risk: Welsh Government has its own fiscal constraints; this takes 3–4 months to establish — too slow for businesses that are failing now.

**Option C — Negotiate flexible interpretation of the 30% threshold.** Work with the fund administrators to interpret "Tata exposure" to include indirect exposure (a cleaning firm that lost 28% of its revenue because its primary customer — a steel equipment supplier — went under due to the Tata closure). This could extend coverage to 10–12 of the 15 excluded businesses without a formal threshold change. Risk: flexible interpretation creates inconsistency; businesses that were refused under the strict threshold may seek judicial review.

---

## The call

Escalate for more funding and lower threshold, create a parallel Welsh fund, or negotiate flexible interpretation of current criteria?

## Tension

Speed (businesses are failing now) vs political capital (asking government twice) vs legal risk (flexible interpretation invites challenge).
