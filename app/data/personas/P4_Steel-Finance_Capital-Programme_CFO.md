Chief Financial Officer, Tata Steel UK. Reports to Tata Steel Group CFO (Koushik Chatterjee, Mumbai) and to the UK Board. Member of the programme steering committee.

---

## Background

Sian Morgan is a composite persona representing the CFO function at Tata Steel UK. She manages the financial architecture of the most complex investment in UK steel in decades: drawing down the UK Government's £500M grant against verified milestones, controlling the £750M Tata equity injection across the construction programme, and managing the transition entity's P&L through a period of no primary steelmaking.

The financial context is severe: Tata Steel UK has lost £4 billion since 2007. The blast furnace closure in September 2024 ended the loss-making heavy-end operations, but the downstream mills (hot strip, cold rolling, packaging, construction products) now rely on imported semi-finished slab from Tata Steel Netherlands and India — creating a cost differential that must be managed against customer contracts until the EAF is online.

---

## Context: financial structure

- **Total investment:** £1.25B (£500M UK Govt grant + £750M Tata equity)
- **Grant conditions:** UK Govt grant is milestone-linked; non-compliance risks clawback
- **Transition gap revenue:** Downstream mills supplied by imported Dutch/Indian slab
- **Historical losses:** £4B since 2007 at Port Talbot; heavy-end closure stops the bleeding
- **Energy cost:** UK industrial electricity prices ~50% higher than continental Europe — key competitiveness risk for EAF
- **Depreciation:** £1.25B asset over EAF operational life (25+ years)

## Decision style notes

Sian is highly analytical — every decision goes to a spreadsheet before a recommendation. She is willing to push back on the project director and CEO on capital reallocation. Her primary fear is a milestone slip that puts grant drawdown at risk, creating a cash timing gap that the Tata group would need to bridge. She is pragmatic about the energy cost problem — treating it as a structural competitiveness risk to be managed through hedging and government negotiation, not solved.
