# P10 · Priya Mehta

**Domain:** Steel Commercial / Automotive Supply
**Platform:** Automotive & Advanced Manufacturing Customer Relationships
**Persona:** Commercial Director, Tata Steel UK

---

## Role

Commercial Director, Tata Steel UK — Automotive and Advanced Manufacturing. Reports to the CEO. Owns the customer-facing revenue side of the EAF business case: securing long-term offtake agreements that justify the investment and maintaining continuity through the 2025–2027 transition gap.

---

## Background

Priya Mehta is a composite persona representing the commercial function that is, in many ways, the ultimate test of the entire £1.25B investment. The EAF produces financial returns only if customers buy the steel it makes — at a price that covers operating costs, repays capital, and provides a margin.

JLR (Jaguar Land Rover) has already signed a memorandum of understanding with Tata Steel for green steel supply from Port Talbot after the EAF transformation — the most significant commercial signal from the project. Priya's job is to convert that MOU into a signed multi-year offtake agreement, and to replicate it with BMW, Stellantis, and aerospace customers before 2027.

The steel grade challenge is live: EAF steel is technically suitable for approximately 90% of automotive grades. The remaining 10% — the most demanding electrical steels, ultra-high-strength automotive grades, and certain packaging steels — still require a virgin iron source, which the EAF alone cannot provide without adding direct reduced iron (DRI/HBI). Priya is selling what the EAF can do while managing customer expectations on what it cannot do yet.

---

## Context: customer landscape

- **JLR:** MOU signed for green steel post-2027; Tata Motors owns JLR — a related-party dynamic
- **BMW:** UK plants (MINI, Rolls-Royce) are significant flat steel customers
- **Stellantis:** Already pushed back on price increases during transition gap
- **Packaging customers:** DS Smith, Crown, Rexnord — require tinplate grades
- **Construction:** Significant volume but lower grade requirements; less green-steel premium
- **Transition gap:** 2025–2027 — customers supplied from imported Dutch/Indian slab at cost premium
- **Green steel premium:** JCB has also signed an MOU — signals industrial customers will pay a premium for verified low-carbon steel

## Decision style notes

Priya is strategically minded but commercially pragmatic. She knows that the JLR MOU is a gift — the related-party relationship means it will convert to a contract — but that every other customer relationship has to be earned purely on commercial merit. She is cautious about overpromising on grade capability before the EAF is commissioned and the metallurgical programme is validated. Her biggest risk is that a competitor (British Steel Scunthorpe, continental producers) picks off a key automotive customer during the 2025–2027 gap when Tata UK cannot supply from domestic primary production.
