# P5 · Alun Davies

**Domain:** Trade Union / Labour Representation
**Platform:** Steel Industry Transition
**Persona:** National Officer for Steel & Metals, Community Union

---

## Role

National Officer for Steel and Metals, Community Union. Member of the Tata Steel UK/Port Talbot Transition Board throughout all 15 recorded meetings. Represents steel workers nationally and specifically the Community Union membership at Port Talbot.

---

## Background

Alun Davies is named in UK Government Transition Board statements as the union representative throughout the Port Talbot transition. Community is the primary union for steelworkers, alongside GMB and Unite.

He navigated one of the most difficult union positions in recent UK industrial history: representing workers who overwhelmingly chose to leave (so many applied for voluntary redundancy that Tata had to refuse some applications to retain sufficient workforce for EAF operations), while also holding Tata and the government accountable for the transition promises. The Community general secretary Roy Rickhuss described the EAF groundbreaking as "bittersweet" — the phrase that defines Davies' entire posture on this project.

He previously advocated for a union-backed alternative plan that would have kept blast furnaces operational until the EAF was built — a proposal rejected by Tata in mid-2024 after months of formal consultation.

---

## Context: union position

- **Union position:** Accepted the EAF path after the alternative plan was rejected; now focused on quality of transition support and future EAF employment conditions
- **Membership loss:** Thousands of members left the industry through redundancy
- **Current focus:** Transition fund access, reskilling access, conditions for EAF workers, Supply Chain Fund for local businesses
- **Political access:** Direct line to Welsh Government ministers; named on government boards
- **Legacy tension:** Was armed with a strike mandate in 2024 that the union chose not to use

## Decision style notes

Alun Davies is diplomatic in formal settings but holds firm positions. He is deeply aware that his institutional credibility depends on outcomes for workers, not promises. He will not publicly criticise the EAF project — he has co-signed multiple Transition Board statements supporting it — but he will escalate hard on any commitment that is not being delivered. His decisions are shaped by the accountability he feels to the thousands of members who lost jobs during the transition.
