## Role

Head of Scrap Procurement and Circular Supply Chain, Tata Steel UK. Reports to the CFO and COO. Based at Port Talbot with regular travel to UK scrap merchants and processors.

---

## Background

Kate Ellison is a composite persona representing the most strategically critical and least publicly visible role in the Port Talbot EAF project. She must secure approximately 2 million tonnes of high-quality UK-sourced scrap steel per year before the EAF is commissioned in late 2027 — a 70% increase in demand from a market that currently exports most of its material.

The UK exports 8 million tonnes of scrap annually — more than any country except the USA. Most of this is low-grade or mixed quality, processed by overseas facilities with lower costs and mature circular steel economies, and often returned to the UK as finished goods that undercut domestic manufacturers. EAFs are sensitive to contaminants (copper, tin, and residuals degrade steel quality); the scrap Kate needs is not the same scrap the UK currently sends abroad.

A December 2025 UK Steel Circular Steel Sub-Committee report warned that without investment in UK scrap processing infrastructure, the country risks being unable to secure the high-quality feedstock needed for EAF transition.

---

## Context: scrap market dynamics

- **EAF annual requirement:** ~2M tonnes high-quality (low-residual) scrap
- **UK scrap surplus:** 8M tonnes/yr exported — but mostly lower grade
- **Quality challenge:** EAF steel can contain up to 90% scrap (vs 30% in BF) but requires strict residual limits
- **Competition:** Every European EAF operator is competing for the same high-quality scrap
- **Infrastructure gap:** UK scrap shredding and sorting capacity is underdeveloped vs Germany, Netherlands
- **Transport:** Scrap will arrive at Port Talbot primarily by rail (existing network)
- **JCB commitment:** JCB signed an MOU with Tata for green steel supply — creating a commercial incentive to prioritise UK automotive scrap loops

## Decision style notes

Kate is a pragmatic operator in a market that is fundamentally relationship-driven. Scrap merchants are mostly SMEs; long-term contracts are unusual in this sector. She is building a new procurement model — treating scrap as a strategic feedstock rather than a commodity afterthought — which requires changing the mindset of both Tata and the UK scrap industry simultaneously. Her decisions tend toward strategic when negotiating infrastructure partnerships, pragmatic when making volume vs cost trade-offs.
